# Structure-Aware Hybrid Retrieval Augmentation for Traditional Chinese Medicine Question Answering on Public TCM-QG

Liheng Chen, Ling Cao

Chongqing University of Posts and Telecommunications, Software Engineering

## Abstract

Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) question answering systems require not only fluent generation, but also reliable evidence, traceable citations, and controllable risk. To reduce factual drift and weak evidence grounding in large language model based professional QA, this paper reports the V1.6 public TCM-QG experiment of a structure-aware hybrid retrieval augmentation method. The method combines BM25, BGE-M3 dense retrieval, reciprocal rank fusion, and a BGE reranker with a Parent-Child evidence organization strategy. It first retrieves fine-grained child chunks and then expands matched evidence to parent context before answer generation. The frozen experiment uses the public Tianchi TCM-QG source, with 18,456 normalized QA pairs and 2,786 test questions. Results show that the complete Parent-Child method does not provide a stable answer-level advantage over the strong hybrid-rerank chunk baseline B4: the P-B4 Char F1 delta is 0.0033 with a 95% confidence interval of [-0.0033, 0.0100]. However, parent context has a stable contribution over the child-only ablation. Compared with P-no-parent, P improves Char F1 by 0.0149 and citation recall by 0.1350, with positive lower bounds in both 95% confidence intervals. A human blind review further reports 74.06% evidence support, 74.06% citation correctness, and 0 hallucination rate on the sampled rows. The experiment suggests that structured parent context is better interpreted as a component for improving evidence coverage and traceability, rather than as proof that Parent-Child RAG is globally superior to a strong hybrid-rerank baseline under the current setting.

**Keywords:** Traditional Chinese Medicine question answering; retrieval-augmented generation; hybrid retrieval; Parent-Child; evidence traceability

## 1 Introduction

Large language models have substantially improved natural language understanding, dialogue interaction, and answer generation. In medical and TCM scenarios, however, a useful answer must be more than fluent. It must be factually reliable, supported by evidence, and safe enough for professional use. TCM QA is especially sensitive to fine-grained relations among terms, formulas, syndromes, and source documents. A system that relies only on model parameters or prompt engineering can suffer from outdated knowledge, hallucination, and opaque evidence provenance.

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) reduces these risks by retrieving external documents before answer generation. Most RAG systems split documents into fixed-length chunks and retrieve over these chunks using sparse, dense, or hybrid retrieval. This design is effective for local evidence matching, but it creates a granularity trade-off in TCM QA. Fine-grained chunks improve matching precision but may break the original context; longer contexts preserve semantic completeness but may add noise and weaken ranking precision. Balancing fine-grained retrieval with higher-level semantic context is therefore a key issue for evidence-grounded TCM QA.

This paper focuses on the experimentally validated part of the thesis proposal, namely hybrid retrieval augmentation. The multi-agent collaboration module is not claimed as an experimental contribution here because it was not evaluated in the V1.6 public TCM-QG experiment. The contributions are threefold. First, we implement a structure-aware Parent-Child evidence organization strategy that combines child-level retrieval with parent-level context expansion. Second, we conduct a frozen public TCM-QG experiment covering preregistration, indexing, retrieval, real LLM answer generation, automatic metrics, paired bootstrap, and human blind review. Third, we provide a conservative interpretation boundary through comparisons with a strong chunk baseline and a child-only ablation.

## 2 Method

The key idea is to decouple retrieval granularity from answer evidence granularity. For the ordinary chunk baseline B4, documents are split into fixed 500/80 overlap chunks. BM25, BGE-M3 dense retrieval, reciprocal rank fusion (RRF), and a BGE reranker are applied to this chunk collection.

For the Parent-Child method P, the system indexes finer child chunks. It first retrieves and reranks child chunks to locate local evidence relevant to the question. The matched child chunks are then mapped back to their parent document contexts. After deduplication, the parent contexts are provided to the answer model. This design preserves the precision of child-level matching while exposing the answer model to more complete semantic context.

To isolate the contribution of parent context, we include a P-no-parent ablation. P-no-parent uses exactly the same child ranking as P but provides only child chunk text to the answer model. Thus, the difference between P and P-no-parent mainly measures the impact of parent expansion on evidence coverage, citation behavior, and answer quality. A B0 no-evidence baseline is also included to observe answer behavior without retrieved evidence.

The formal retrieval matrix contains seven configurations: B1 is BM25, B2 is BGE-M3 dense retrieval, B3 is BM25 plus dense retrieval with RRF, B4 is hybrid retrieval with reranking, P is child retrieval with parent context, P-no-parent is the child-only ablation, and P-no-reranker removes reranking for diagnosis. To control cost and keep the answer-level narrative focused, only B0, B4, P, and P-no-parent are run in the answer matrix.

## 3 Experimental Design

The experiment uses the public Tianchi TCM-QG local source file `train.json`. After normalization, the dataset contains 18,456 QA pairs, including 12,956 training-pool pairs, 2,714 development questions, and 2,786 test questions. The experiment code is isolated under `experiments/rag_v1_6`. Large generated artifacts are stored locally under `data/rag_v1_6`; committable manifests only contain hashes, counts, and aggregate metrics, without raw text, questions, reference answers, or generated answers.

The retrieval stack uses BAAI/bge-m3 for dense embeddings and BAAI/bge-reranker-v2-m3 for reranking. The answer model is `deepseek-chat`, with temperature 0, one repeat, max_tokens 256, and top 5 evidence contexts. The prompt requires the model to answer only from the given public-document evidence, abstain when evidence is insufficient, and output JSON containing the answer and citations.

Metrics are organized into three levels. Retrieval is evaluated by Doc Recall@5, Answer Span Hit@5, and Answer Span Coverage@5. Automatic answer evaluation uses exact match, character F1, ROUGE-L F1, citation recall, unsupported answer rate, and abstain rate. Statistical testing uses paired bootstrap with `source_doc_id` as the resampling unit and 10,000 resamples. Human blind review covers 160 main-review questions and four methods, yielding 640 reviewed rows; 64 rows are second-reviewed, and 120 questions are additionally sampled for the parent-ablation focus set.

## 4 Results

Table 1 reports test-set retrieval results. P slightly outperforms B4 in Doc Recall@5 and Answer Span Hit@5, reaching 0.6360 and 0.7563 respectively. P and P-no-parent share the same child ranking, so their Doc Recall@5 values are identical. P has higher Answer Span Coverage@5 than P-no-parent, suggesting that parent context improves the availability of answer-bearing evidence.

| Method | Doc Recall@5 | Answer Span Hit@5 | Answer Span Coverage@5 |

| B1 BM25 | 0.5919 | 0.7143 | 0.5919 |
| B2 Dense | 0.5108 | 0.6342 | 0.5107 |
| B3 Hybrid | 0.5894 | 0.7118 | 0.5893 |
| B4 Hybrid+Rerank | 0.6332 | 0.7516 | 0.6331 |
| P Parent-Child | 0.6360 | 0.7563 | 0.6360 |
| P-no-parent | 0.6360 | 0.6285 | 0.5963 |
| P-no-reranker | 0.5847 | 0.7125 | 0.5847 |

Table 2 reports automatic answer metrics from the real LLM answer run. P is numerically higher than B4 in Char F1, ROUGE-L F1, and citation recall, and lower in unsupported answer rate. However, these numerical differences must be interpreted with the bootstrap intervals.

| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| B0 | 0.0018 | 0.2272 | 0.1705 | N/A | N/A |
| B4 | 0.2014 | 0.5892 | 0.5691 | 0.8238 | 0.1665 |

| P-no-parent | 0.1809 | 0.5776 | 0.5584 | 0.6960 | 0.2886 |

The paired bootstrap shows that B4 is clearly better than B0, with a Char F1 delta of 0.3620 and a 95% confidence interval of [0.3458, 0.3783]. The P-B4 Char F1 delta is 0.0033 with a 95% confidence interval of [-0.0033, 0.0100], while the P-B4 citation recall delta is 0.0072 with a 95% confidence interval of [-0.0032, 0.0174]. Because both intervals cross zero, we cannot claim that P is stably better than B4 at the answer level. In contrast, the P versus P-no-parent comparison is positive. P improves Char F1 by 0.0149 with a 95% confidence interval of [0.0073, 0.0226], improves citation recall by 0.1350 with a 95% confidence interval of [0.1206, 0.1497], and reduces unsupported answer rate by 0.1317.

The human blind review is consistent with this interpretation. Among 640 main-review rows, the answer correctness rate is 69.38%, the evidence-supported rate is 74.06%, the citation correctness rate is 74.06%, the hallucination rate is 0, and the clinical safety issue rate is 0.47%. The second review reports no disagreement. The human review cannot turn the uncertain P-B4 automatic comparison into a strong success claim, but it supports the narrower claim that the method mainly contributes to evidence support and citation traceability.

## 5 Discussion

The results indicate that Parent-Child evidence organization makes a local but meaningful contribution in the public TCM-QG setting. Parent context improves evidence expansion, citation recall, and unsupported answer rate over the child-only condition. At the same time, the complete P method has not shown a stable answer-level advantage over the strong B4 hybrid-rerank baseline. This distinction is important for scientific reporting: the experimentally supported contribution is structured evidence organization for traceability and coverage, not global superiority over ordinary chunk-based RAG.

There are several possible reasons why P does not significantly outperform B4. First, many TCM-QG answers are concise, and ordinary 500/80 chunks may already contain enough evidence. Second, parent context can provide useful background, but it may also introduce question-irrelevant text and increase the answer model's evidence selection burden. Third, the current parent expansion strategy mainly relies on deduplication and top-k control; it does not yet include sentence-level filtering, evidence compression, or structured summarization.

Therefore, this paper interprets the V1.6 result as valid ablation evidence for an evidence organization module in a TCM QA system. Future work should explore shorter parent units, evidence compression, multi-agent verification, and safety review agents, so that the broader system-level collaboration proposed in the thesis can be evaluated by controlled experiments rather than described only as an architecture.

## 6 Conclusion

This paper evaluates a structure-aware hybrid retrieval augmentation method for TCM QA using the frozen TCM-Flow V1.6 public TCM-QG experiment. The complete Parent-Child method does not stably outperform the strong hybrid-rerank chunk baseline under the current formal test. However, parent context significantly improves Char F1, citation recall, and unsupported answer rate over the child-only ablation. These findings suggest that structured parent context is valuable for evidence coverage and traceability, while further context-noise control and evidence compression are needed before claiming global superiority. The experiment provides reproducible evidence and a safer writing boundary for subsequent TCM RAG QA research.
